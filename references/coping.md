# Coping — Emotional Pacing Through a Job Search

> **Purpose:** Job searching is a grief-adjacent process (repeated rejection, uncertain timeline, identity hits). The rest of this skill focuses on execution; this file addresses the part that makes execution sustainable. Load this reference when the candidate is post-rejection, pre-interview, in a high-volume stretch, or showing burnout signals.
>
> **Scope:** practical protocols only. This is not therapy and not a substitute for mental-health care. If the candidate mentions self-harm, hopelessness lasting weeks, or inability to function, step out of job-search mode and recommend they talk to a professional or trusted person.

## Part 1 — The 24-Hour Rejection Protocol

**Rule:** Do not submit a new application to a *different* company within 24 hours of receiving a rejection from a role the candidate was invested in (High Match, made it past initial screen, or had an interview).

**Why:** Rejection-driven applications are lower quality — the candidate is applying to soothe the sting, not to match fit. They skip research, skip customisation, and the next rejection compounds the original one.

**The protocol:**

1. **Acknowledge, don't analyse immediately.** If the candidate shares the rejection, say so briefly. Do not reflexively pivot to "here's what went wrong." Post-mortem comes later.
2. **Name what was invested.** "You prepped 3 STAR stories and wrote a custom cover letter" — naming the effort separates effort from outcome.
3. **Defer the post-mortem by 24 hours.** If the candidate is already asking "what do I change in my resume" within the first hour, suggest: "Let's sit with this until tomorrow. You'll analyse more clearly with 24h of distance."
4. **One small restorative action tonight.** Not a new application. Examples: close the laptop, take a walk, call a friend, watch something light. The goal is to break the ruminate → refresh inbox → ruminate loop.
5. **Next day: short post-mortem.** Use the Phase 9 diagnostic framework (targeting · resume · volume · other filters) *if* the candidate is willing. If not, defer another day.

**What this protects against:**

- Applying to a clearly-wrong role "just to have something in the pipeline" → zero hit rate, damages confidence further.
- Submitting a lazy copy-paste cover letter → lowers the candidate's personal standard, then becomes their new default.
- Starting a panic revision of the master resume based on a single N=1 data point.

**Exception:** if the candidate is in an urgent timeline (unemployed with < 4 weeks of runway), skip the 24-hour pause — but still avoid submitting lazy applications. The bar is "is this application higher quality than the one that just got rejected?"

## Part 2 — Burnout Early Warning

Track these signals. Any two concurrent = slow down. Three or more = stop new applications for 48 hours and re-plan.

| Signal | Healthy range | Warning |
|---|---|---|
| Applications per week | 5–12 (quality-focused) | > 15 sustained for 2+ weeks |
| Sleep | 7–9 hours | < 6 hours for 3+ nights |
| "Can I take today off?" | Monthly is fine | Daily thought, followed by guilt |
| Resume rewrites | 1–2 per month | Rewriting every few days chasing feedback loops |
| Emotional flatness when describing the target role | — | "I don't even know why I'd want this job anymore" |
| Physical symptoms | — | Headaches, chest tightness, stomach issues tied to inbox checks |

**If triggered:**

1. **Name it to the candidate.** "You applied to 22 roles this week on <6h of sleep — that's the burnout pattern, not the productive one."
2. **48-hour no-new-applications window.** Existing applications in pipeline still get replied to (interview invites, follow-ups) — the pause is only on net-new submissions.
3. **Downshift volume target.** If the previous plan was "20/week", the new target is 5–8/week for the next 2 weeks. Fewer, better-fit, better-customised.
4. **Re-examine the targeting.** Burnout often signals the candidate is applying too broadly (every "vaguely relevant" role) or too narrowly (only dream-tier roles with no Medium Match realism). Both correlate with low hit rates, which compound fatigue.
5. **Sleep debt is the top lever.** If sleep < 6h for 3+ nights, protect one full evening before any new application — interview performance tanks at sleep-deprivation levels long before the candidate notices.

**What this protects against:**

- "Spray and pray" volume phases that produce 0 interviews and 3 weeks of lost confidence.
- Candidates writing rejection narratives into identity ("I'm unhirable") based on a pattern that is actually fatigue-driven, not skills-driven.
- Interview no-shows and bomb-outs when fatigue hits right as an opportunity arrives.

## Part 3 — Pre-Interview Stabilization (30 Minutes)

Run with the candidate in the 30 minutes before an interview. This is not prep — prep was done in Phase 10. This is nervous-system regulation so the prep can actually surface under pressure.

**T–30 to T–20:**
- Close all other tabs and notifications.
- Re-read the top of the JD and the candidate's Summary paragraph — one page each. Nothing new.
- Re-read the top 3 STAR stories the candidate already prepared. Do not add a fourth now.

**T–20 to T–10:**
- Stand up. Walk. Do something physical — this drains the stress-hormone peak that otherwise lands in the first 5 minutes of the call.
- If comfortable: 4-count box breathing (in 4, hold 4, out 4, hold 4) for 2 minutes. Slows heart rate.
- Drink water. Dry mouth on video calls is audible.

**T–10 to T–5:**
- Tech check: camera on, mic on, background quiet, ring light/window light okay, notes visible off-camera.
- Keep one-page reference visible: the candidate's top 3 metrics and 2 questions they'll ask.

**T–5 to T–0:**
- One confidence anchor. The candidate picks one past specific professional moment when they did something well — not vague, not "I'm great at X", a single 30-second memory. Replay it mentally. This primes the same state they want to show up in.
- Join early. Sitting in the waiting room for 1 minute is better than rushing in at T–0.

**Anti-pattern to warn about:**

- Reading the entire resume / full JD in the final 30 minutes — saturates working memory, makes the first question feel like a pop quiz.
- Caffeine spike in the last hour — amplifies the cortisol curve, produces jitter, not energy.
- Rehearsing answers word-for-word — sounds scripted, and any deviation in the real question throws the whole memorised track off.

## Part 4 — Offer Anxiety: What's Actually Real

When an offer arrives, candidates commonly talk themselves out of negotiating, or accept too fast, out of three fears. The candidate deserves the actual data:

**Fear 1: "They'll rescind the offer if I counter."**
Rescission after a polite, professional counter is rare — real rate is meaningfully under 5% in normal commercial hiring. It happens almost exclusively when:
(a) the counter is aggressive relative to the posted range (> 20% above),
(b) the tone is adversarial or ultimatum-based, or
(c) the candidate misrepresented something in the process.
A standard counter ("Thank you — I'm enthusiastic. Based on my experience with X, I was hoping for $Y. Is there flexibility?") does not trigger rescission.

**Fear 2: "They'll think I'm greedy / difficult."**
Recruiters negotiate every day. They expect a counter from strong candidates — the candidates who accept immediately are sometimes read as *less* confident in their value. What is read as "greedy" is rudeness, not the counter itself.

**Fear 3: "If I ask for time to decide, they'll go to another candidate."**
Standard deliberation is 3–5 business days. Employers who refuse any deliberation time are signalling culture problems, not urgency — that signal itself is useful information. Ask for the time.

**Anti-pattern to warn about:**

- Accepting within the same call/email the offer was sent. The offer will still be there in 48 hours.
- Negotiating against a comparable offer the candidate doesn't actually have. If caught, that ends the process.
- Negotiating only base salary when the gap is in total comp. Non-base levers (signing bonus, equity, PTO, start date, remote flex, title) often have more room.

Full counter scripts live in `salary-negotiation.md`. This section only covers the emotional side.

## Part 5 — Pacing Across a Multi-Week Search

Most job searches take 2–6 months end-to-end in 2026 commercial markets (varies widely by role / level / geography). The candidate needs a pacing model that survives that long, not a sprint that dies in 3 weeks.

**Weekly rhythm that holds up:**

- **Monday:** plan the week. 2–3 target applications, not 10. Re-read yesterday's rejections *briefly* and close them.
- **Tuesday–Thursday:** execution days. Custom resumes, company research, apply, log.
- **Friday:** follow-ups, tracker cleanup, interview prep for next week. No new applications.
- **Weekend:** at least one full day off. No inbox checks, no "one more application". This is protective, not lazy — Monday performance depends on it.

**Monthly rhythm:**

- Every 4 weeks: run Phase 9 diagnostics honestly. If response rate is < 10%, the problem is upstream (targeting, resume, market) — increasing volume won't fix it. Fix the upstream problem before returning to volume.
- Every 4 weeks: one full weekend off. No job-search activity. The search is a second job and second jobs need the same recovery time as first ones.

**Red flag to name to the candidate:**

- Working 7 days a week for 3+ weeks in job search mode. This correlates with declining application quality and declining interview performance. It is not more effective than 5 days.
- Measuring self-worth by weekly application count. The count is an input, not an outcome. Outcome metrics are interview invites and offers.

## What this reference does NOT do

- It does not replace therapy. If the candidate describes symptoms persisting for weeks (hopelessness, inability to function, loss of interest in everything), Claude should step out of job-search mode and suggest the candidate talk to a professional or trusted person.
- It does not provide diagnosis or clinical advice.
- It does not make claims about medication, specific mental-health conditions, or crisis intervention.

The coping protocols here are pragmatic pacing rules — the kind a good mentor would share. They are not a substitute for actual mental-health support when it's needed.
