# Salary Negotiation

Most candidates leave money on the table by accepting the first number offered. Negotiation is expected — it does not jeopardise offers unless handled poorly.

---

## Before Applying: Know Your Numbers

Establish three figures before any application:

| Figure | Definition |
|---|---|
| **Floor** | The minimum you would accept (do not disclose) |
| **Target** | What you actually want (anchor slightly above this) |
| **Anchor** | What you open with — 10–15% above target |

**Research sources** (in order of reliability):
1. Glassdoor / Levels.fyi / LinkedIn Salary (filter by location, company size, years of experience)
2. Direct conversations with peers in similar roles
3. Job postings that list salary ranges (increasingly common in Ontario due to Pay Transparency Act)
4. `get_company_data` (if connected) for salary data by role

---

## When They Ask "What Are Your Salary Expectations?"

### In the application form
If the field is optional, leave it blank. If required, enter your anchor range (e.g. "$75,000–$90,000"). Never enter a single number.

### In an HR screen
Delay if possible:
> "I'm still learning about the full scope of the role — could you share what the budgeted range is for this position?"

If they push for a number:
> "Based on my research and experience, I'm targeting somewhere in the $[anchor range] range, though I'm flexible depending on the total package."

### Never:
- Give your floor
- Say "I'm flexible" without any number (they will anchor low)
- Apologise for your number

---

## Evaluating an Offer

An offer is not just base salary. Evaluate the total package:

| Component | What to check |
|---|---|
| Base salary | vs. your target and market data |
| Bonus structure | % of base, frequency, guaranteed vs. discretionary |
| Benefits | Health/dental, pension/RRSP match, paid time off |
| Remote / flexibility | Days in office, flexibility hours |
| Equity | Options vs. RSUs, vesting schedule, strike price |
| Growth path | Title, promotion timeline, development budget |
| Probation period | Length and what it affects |

Express enthusiasm for the offer before negotiating. Never negotiate via email if you can do it by phone or in person.

---

## How to Counter an Offer

**Standard counter (base salary):**
> "Thank you so much — I'm really excited about the role and the team. The offer is close to where I was hoping to land, but based on my research and the scope of what you've described, I was expecting something in the [$X–$Y] range. Is there any flexibility there?"

**If base is firm, negotiate other elements:**
> "I understand base may be fixed at this level. Is there flexibility on [signing bonus / additional vacation days / remote work days / professional development budget]?"

**If the offer is well below expectations:**
> "I really appreciate the offer, and I'm genuinely interested in the role. The compensation is lower than I'd expected based on my research — would it be possible to revisit? I'd hate for compensation to be the reason we don't move forward."

---

## After Negotiating

- Get the final offer in writing before giving notice anywhere
- Ask for a deadline to respond (standard is 3–5 business days; request an extension if needed)
- Once accepted, confirm start date and any pre-employment requirements in writing

---

## When Not to Negotiate

- Contract roles with a fixed published rate
- Government / public sector positions with fixed salary bands (the band is the band)
- When the offer already meets or exceeds your anchor

Even in these cases, you can ask about non-salary elements (flex, training budget, start date).
