# JD Analysis & Keyword Optimisation

## Step 1 — Categorise Every Requirement

Read the full JD and sort every requirement into:

| Category | Signals |
|---|---|
| **Hard requirements** | "required", "must have", "X years of Y experience required", named certifications |
| **Preferred / bonus** | "nice to have", "asset", "preferred", "plus" |
| **Implicit requirements** | Tools named in responsibilities without "required" label (if listed 3+ times, treat as required) |
| **Exclusion triggers** | Age/eligibility restrictions, geography the candidate cannot meet, mandatory certs they don't have |

---

## Step 2 — Immediate Exclusion Criteria

Recommend removing the job from the list (without asking) if:
- Requires a named tool with 5+ years of experience the candidate does not have
- Government youth programme / age-restricted posting the candidate is ineligible for
- Geography requirement the candidate has explicitly excluded
- Mandatory credential (professional licence, security clearance level) the candidate does not hold

Flag and ask the candidate before proceeding if:
- Part-time when full-time was requested (and vice versa)
- Experience gap > 3 years between JD requirement and candidate's actual experience
- Contract / seasonal / fixed-term when permanent was preferred
- Relocation required with no remote option

---

## Step 3 — Keyword Matching Matrix

Build a quick table for every role before writing the resume:

| JD Keyword | In Resume? | Action |
|---|---|---|
| Exact tool name (e.g. "GA4") | ✅ — but uses "Google Analytics" | Replace with exact JD term |
| Skill present, phrased differently | ✅ | Rephrase bullet to use JD's exact wording |
| Skill genuinely absent | ❌ | Note the gap; do **not** add it to resume |
| Soft skill / attribute ("ownership") | ✅ implied | Surface explicitly in summary or bullet |

---

## Step 4 — Summary Formula

Adapt to the candidate and role. Structure:

```
[Role-matched title] with [X]+ years of [primary domain].
[Strongest relevant achievement: metric + method].
[Optional: location note / language note if market-relevant.]
```

**Guiding principles:**
- The title in the summary should echo the job title or something close to it — recruiters pattern-match on this
- Lead with the most impressive number you can honestly claim (revenue, traffic, growth, team size, budget)
- Keep total length to 2 sentences for compact resumes, 3 max for generous versions
- Do not use adjectives like "passionate", "dynamic", "results-driven" without a metric to back them up

**Category-specific patterns:**

*Technical / Dev role:*
"[Stack] Developer with [X]+ years building [type of product] for measurable results. Delivered [metric] through [method] — [context if needed]."

*Marketing / Growth role:*
"[Marketing title] with [X]+ years of [agency/in-house] experience across [channels]. Proven track record driving [metric] across [scope]."

*Senior / Leadership role:*
"[Senior title] with [X]+ years leading [function]. Delivered [metric] through full-funnel strategy and [method]. [Location] based."

*Mixed / generalist role:*
"[Combined title] with [X]+ years of experience managing [primary activity] and [secondary activity]. [Top metric] through [approach]."

---

## Step 5 — Skills Row Prioritisation

**Primary rule**: The most JD-relevant row goes first.

**Within each row**: surface skills that appear in the JD first; de-prioritise or omit skills not mentioned in the JD if the row is already long.

**When to move skills between rows:**
- If the SEO & Marketing row is overflowing, move less-critical analytics tools to the Tools row
- If the Development row is overflowing, move design tools to a Tools or Design row
- "Familiar with X" skills → move to end of row or omit if adding clutter

---

## Step 5b — Keyword Placement (natural, not stuffed)

**Bust the density myth first.** Modern ATSs (Workday, Greenhouse, Lever, Ashby) parse the resume into structured fields — they do **not** rank candidates on how many times a keyword appears. The real screening happens when a recruiter skims the document. What matters:

1. Each top keyword appears **at least once**, in a place a recruiter reads (Summary or a work bullet)
2. Phrasing matches the JD's exact terminology (e.g. "GA4" when JD says GA4, not "Google Analytics")
3. No stuffing — the same keyword forced into 4 sections reads as low-signal to both humans and modern relevance models

**Where a keyword lives, in order of recruiter visibility:**

| Location | Why it matters |
|---|---|
| Job title / Summary | First thing read; signals role alignment |
| Work experience bullet | Primary evidence that the skill was actually used |
| Skills section | Useful for completeness, but Skills-only mentions tend to be skimmed |
| Education | Only relevant if the keyword is a degree / credential |

**How to apply:**
1. Identify the 3–5 most-weighted keywords in the JD (appear in requirements AND responsibilities)
2. For each, confirm it appears in the Summary OR a work bullet — not just the Skills row
3. If it's already in both Summary and a bullet, do not add it elsewhere — you're done

**Example:**
JD keyword: "Google Analytics 4 / GA4"
- Currently only in Skills: → Add to the most relevant work bullet: "…tracked via GA4 and…"
- Already in a bullet: → Do not also force into Summary unless it genuinely belongs in the positioning line

**Do not force keywords unnaturally.** If a keyword cannot be added without misrepresenting the candidate's work, leave it in the Skills row only and note the gap. Honest absence beats dishonest presence.

---

## Step 6 — Work Experience Bullet Adjustment

Only adjust bullets when a JD keyword is absent AND the experience genuinely covers it.

**How to adjust without fabricating:**
- Add context: "via [method that includes the JD term]"
- Reorder emphasis: move the most JD-relevant part of the bullet to the front
- Use the exact JD terminology as a synonym for what's already there (e.g. "content strategy" instead of "editorial planning" if both are accurate)

**What not to do:**
- Do not add a tool the candidate has never used, even as a minor mention
- Do not claim a level of proficiency higher than the candidate's actual experience
- Do not remove metrics to make room for keyword stuffing

---

## Step 7 — Application Form Red Flags

Watch for these during form completion:

| Question type | Guidance |
|---|---|
| "Years of [specific tool] experience" | Answer to the specific tool, not a general category |
| "Do you require sponsorship?" | Answer based on candidate's actual work authorisation status |
| "Portfolio / work samples" | Use only URLs to real, live work the candidate actually produced |
| Skill-specific questions (e.g. "Have you used X?") | Answer truthfully; never fabricate |
| Knock-out questions (salary, location, clearance) | Confirm with candidate before answering; a wrong answer may auto-reject |

---

## Step 8 — Email Application Structure

Use this when the application method is "send us a note / email":

```
Hi [team/name if known],

[1 sentence: most relevant experience + metric, specific to this role]
[1 sentence: evidence — specific URLs or named projects, not "see my portfolio"]

[1 sentence: brief differentiator or why this role specifically]

[Name]
[Phone] | [Email]
```

Length: under 120 words. No formal cover letter preamble ("I am writing to express my interest in..."). No bulleted list of skills. End with name and contact only — no "I look forward to hearing from you" filler.
