# Cover Letter & Cover Material Guide

## When to Write What

| Situation | Format | Length |
|---|---|---|
| JD explicitly says "include a cover letter" | Formal cover letter | 3–4 paragraphs, ~300–400 words |
| Senior / competitive / creative role (even without explicit request) | Formal cover letter | 3–4 paragraphs |
| Application portal has a "message / note / additional info" field | Short professional note | 80–120 words |
| Email application ("email your resume to...") | Professional email with inline note | 80–120 words |
| Indeed / LinkedIn Easy Apply, no text field | None — resume does the work | — |
| Government / non-profit roles | Always write a formal cover letter | 3–4 paragraphs |

**Default rule**: When in doubt, write one. A good cover letter can only help; the absence of one when one was expected can eliminate the application.

---

## Formal Cover Letter Structure

### Opening (1 paragraph)
- **Do not start with**: "I am writing to express my interest in..."
- **Do start with**: Something specific about the company (Phase 4 research) + a clear statement of who the candidate is and why they're relevant

> "When [Company] announced [recent development], I saw an immediate connection to the work I've been doing at [Current/Recent Role] — [brief description]. As a [title] with [X] years of [domain], I'd like to be considered for the [Role] position."

### Body (1–2 paragraphs)
- **Paragraph 1**: Your strongest, most relevant achievement — the story behind the numbers. Not "I grew traffic by 5×" but *how and why* that happened.
- **Paragraph 2** (optional): A second proof point that addresses a different requirement from the JD, or explains a transition / gap if one exists.

The body should answer: *"What specifically qualifies this person for this particular role at this particular company right now?"*

### Closing (1 paragraph)
- Express genuine interest (be specific — reference one thing about the role or company)
- State a clear ask: "I'd welcome the chance to discuss this further" or "I'm available for a call any time this week"
- Do **not** use: "I look forward to hearing from you at your earliest convenience"

## Short Note / Email Template

Use when the format is a portal text field or email application:

```
Hi [Name / "the hiring team"],

[1–2 sentences: most relevant experience + metric, specific to this role and company]
[1 sentence: evidence — specific URL or named project, not "see my portfolio"]
[1 sentence: brief differentiator or genuine interest signal]

Resume attached. Happy to share more.

[Name]
[Phone] | [Email]
```

**Length target**: 80–120 words. This is not a cover letter — it is a reason to open the resume.

## What Makes Cover Letters Fail

| Mistake | Why HR notices |
|---|---|
| Generic opening ("I am writing to express...") | Signals template, not effort |
| Repeating the resume verbatim | Waste of HR's time; no added signal |
| "I am a hardworking, motivated team player" | Every candidate says this |
| Addressing the wrong company or role | Copy-paste error — immediate rejection |
| Longer than 1 page | HR rarely reads past page 1 |
| Focused on what the candidate wants ("I am looking for...") | Cover letters should focus on what the candidate offers |
| Vague achievements ("improved performance") | Unverifiable; signals no real ownership |

## Addressing Red Flags in Cover Material

If the candidate has a gap, career pivot, or other resume red flag, the cover letter is the place to address it directly — briefly and confidently.

**Employment gap:**
> "Between [dates], I took time to [reason — caregiving, health, relocation, retraining]. I've since [what changed], and I'm fully committed to bringing [relevant skill] to [Company]."

**Career pivot:**
> "My background in [previous field] gave me [transferable skill], which is directly applicable to [target role] because [specific reason]. I've since [evidence of transition: project, course, portfolio work]."

**Short tenure:**
> "My roles at [companies] were contract-based and project-scoped — I delivered [outcome] within the defined scope, then moved to the next engagement."

Keep the explanation to 1–2 sentences. State it, don't over-justify it.

## Tone by Company Stage

| Stage | Appropriate tone | What to avoid |
|---|---|---|
| Startup | Energetic, direct, self-sufficient | Corporate formality, passive voice |
| Scale-up | Confident, metric-driven, collaborative | Overly casual, "I'm passionate about" filler |
| Enterprise | Professional, structured, process-aware | Too casual, missing the formal conventions |
| Non-profit / mission-driven | Warm, values-aligned, genuine | Over-emphasising salary or career advancement |
| Government | Formal, comprehensive, qualification-focused | Jargon from private sector |
